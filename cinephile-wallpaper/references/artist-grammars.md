# Artist Grammar Presets

Use these presets as distilled poster-design grammars. Do not ask the image model to imitate a living artist directly. Translate each artist/studio/movement into design operations.

## Contemporary Artist Grammar Bank

Use these as distilled visual operations. Do not write "in the style of [living artist]" as the final instruction. For deceased artists, direct historical reference is less risky, but still prefer observable traits over name-dropping.

Important: these presets are not a suitability menu. Do not choose one because it fits a film's topic, rhythm, genre, or tone. Use a preset only after it has been selected by the weighted/random style router, by explicit user request, or by corrective anti-collapse mode. The notes below describe possible bridge material after selection, not reasons to select the preset.

### Warholian Pop System

- repeated face/object grids;
- flat commercial color;
- halftone or screen-print texture;
- celebrity/image-culture critique;
- emotional distance through repetition.

After this preset is selected, it can bridge to fame, media, consumer culture, icons, superheroes, artificial identity, or repeated trauma through repeated-image systems.

### Hockney-Like Spatial Collage

- bright swimming-pool or California color logic;
- fractured perspective;
- photo-collage joins;
- domestic leisure space;
- clear sunlight and graphic planes.

After this preset is selected, it can bridge to memory, desire, place, youth, sunny surfaces with emotional undertow, or fragmented seeing through fractured spatial collage.

### Basquiat-Like Neo-Expressionist Mark System

- raw marks, crowns, skeletal diagrams, crossed-out words as shapes;
- street-wall texture;
- symbolic aggression;
- unstable figure-ground.

After this preset is selected, it can bridge to urban pressure, anger, race/class systems, youth energy, violence, or fractured identity through raw symbolic marks. Avoid using legible text unless the user asked for text in-image.

### Rothko-Like Color Field Pressure

- stacked soft-edged color rectangles;
- immersive emotional atmosphere;
- minimal object content;
- color as metaphysical pressure.

After this preset is selected, it can bridge to atmosphere, grief, waiting, silence, spiritual pressure, or impossible longing through color-field pressure.

### Kandinsky-Like Musical Abstraction

- points, lines, circles, diagonals;

After this preset is selected, it can bridge to music, inner turbulence, city rhythm, cosmic movement, or dream logic through abstract rhythm.

After this preset is selected, it can bridge to science fiction, spiritual austerity, institutional systems, fate, void, or cosmic abstraction through radical geometric reduction.

### Picasso/Braque Cubist Fragment

### Duchamp/Dada Conceptual Displacement

### Beuys/Arte Povera Material Ritual

- felt, fat, ash, rope, copper, earth, worn cloth;

### Yayoi Kusama-Like Pattern Infinity

- repeating dots or nets;
- obsessive pattern field;
- body absorbed by environment.

### Nam June Paik-Like Video Signal

Use for films about technology, memory as media, surveillance, cyberpunk, television, or mediated identity.

## Huang Hai-Inspired Chinese Poster Grammar

### Use When

Use for films that benefit from restraint, poetic symbolism, Eastern aesthetics, emotional compression, mythic imagery, childhood memory, historical weight, martial-arts poetics, social realism, or quiet grandeur.

Works especially well for:

- Chinese-language cinema;
- East Asian art-house films;
- animation with mythic or childhood themes;
- films about memory, fate, sacrifice, family, history, landscape, or spiritual passage;
- films where one symbolic image can carry the whole emotional thesis.

Avoid as default for:

- loud action spectacle;
- glossy superhero marketing;
- comedy posters that need immediate gag energy;
- films requiring dense ensemble star promotion.

### Visual DNA

- **Core method**: compress the film into one poetic symbol rather than illustrating a scene.
- **Composition**: large negative space, one dominant image, asymmetry, vertical tension, quiet center of gravity.
- **Symbol strategy**: an object, body shape, animal, landscape, doorway, window, water surface, shadow, or brush mark becomes the film's emotional thesis.
- **Scale**: small human figures against a vast symbolic environment, or a huge tactile form carrying tiny figures.
- **Palette**: black/white/gray ink worlds; muted earth colors; one restrained accent such as red, gold, cyan, or warm green.
- **Texture**: paper grain, ink wash, brush friction, mist, aged print, painterly matte surfaces.
- **Typography**: sparse, carefully placed, sometimes calligraphic or custom title lettering; text should feel integrated with the image, not pasted on.
- **Emotion**: elegiac, distilled, humane, meditative, culturally rooted, quietly powerful.

### Prompt Translation

Do not write: `in Huang Hai's style`.

Write:

```text
Create an original Chinese art-house film poster key visual. Distill the film
into one poetic symbolic image rather than a literal scene. Use large negative
space, restrained ink-wash atmosphere, paper grain, a muted black-white-gray
palette with one subtle accent color, and a single dominant metaphorical object
or landscape that carries the film's emotional thesis. Small human presence if
needed, quiet asymmetrical composition, elegant cinematic restraint, no text,
no logos, no credits, leave space for later typography.
```

### Stronger Abstract Variant

```text
Create a symbolic poster visual with Eastern poetic restraint: one central
metaphor, large empty space, ink-wash texture, tactile paper grain, minimal
palette, and a tiny human trace absorbed by landscape or object. It should feel
like a film's soul condensed into one image, not a realistic still. No text,
no logos, no credits.
```

### Layout Notes

- Put typography in the quietest region of the image.
- Prefer one title block and one small metadata line.
- Do not crowd the symbol.
- Preserve silence; empty space is part of the image.

### Avoid

- direct copies of known Huang Hai posters;
- literal recreations of famous film scenes;
- generic Chinese ornament;
- overusing dragons, red lanterns, calligraphy, or ink splashes as decoration;
- photorealistic actor portraits;
- dense credits or festival laurels;
- a “tourist China” look.

### Quick Selection Phrase

Use this phrase in a prompt package when the user asks for this preset:

```text
Design grammar: Chinese art-house poster restraint, Huang Hai-inspired symbolic compression, ink-wash negative space, one poetic central metaphor, minimal typography-safe composition.
```
